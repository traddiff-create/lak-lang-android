1. Orthography and Pronunciation

Accurate pronunciation is not optional in Lakota — it determines meaning. The three-way consonant distinctions (plain, aspirated, ejective), the difference between plain h and uvular ȟ, and the proper nasalization of vowels are all meaning-changing. Mixing up these sounds can turn one word into an entirely different one.

↑ contents

1.1 Vowel System

Lakota has five basic oral vowels and three nasal vowels.

Oral Vowels:

Letter IPA Description Example English Approximation
a /a/ Low central táku "father"
e /e/ Mid front hé "bet"
i /i/ High front mní "machine"
o /o/ Mid back čhó "note"
u /u/ High back šúŋka "boot"

Nasal Vowels (marked with ŋ): Air flows through both the mouth and the nose simultaneously — not "vowel + n" but a single nasalized vowel sound. Think of French nasals.

Letter IPA Example French Approximation
aŋ /ã/ haŋpí (moccasins) "enfant"
iŋ /ĩ/ čhiŋ (to want) "vin"
uŋ /ũ/ šúŋka (dog) "un"

Stress: Primary stress is marked with an acute accent (á, é, í, ó, ú). Stress typically falls on the second syllable and is realized as greater length and higher pitch. Examples: wašté (good), táku (what/something), makȟóčhe (land/country).

Plain Stops (Unaspirated) — No puff of air

p /p/ pȟá "spin" (not "pin")
t /t/ táku "stick" (not "tick")
k /k/ ká "skill" (not "kill")
č /tʃ/ čháŋ "church" (unaspirated)

pȟ /pʰ/ pȟehíŋ (hair) Heavy breath after p

kȟ /kʰ/ kȟaŋsú (plum) Heavy breath after k
čȟ /tʃʰ/ čȟó (liver) Heavy breath after č

Ejective Stops — Glottalized, sharp popping release

Letter IPA Example Description
p' /p'/ p'á (bitter) Close glottis, build pressure, pop
t' /t'/ t'á (to die) Sharp glottal release
k' /k'/ k'ú (to give) Glottalized velar
č' /tʃ'/ č'éǧa (drum) Ejective affricate

Fricatives

Letter IPA Example English Approximation
s /s/ siŋté (tail) "sun"
š /ʃ/ šúŋka (dog) "ship"
z /z/ zí (yellow) "zebra"
ž /ʒ/ žaŋ (pine gum) "measure"
x /x/ xá (to uncover) German "Bach"

ȟ /χ/ ȟáŋ (yes, female) Uvular fricative — further back than x

Critical Distinction: h vs. ȟ. The plain h is a simple glottal fricative, like English "hat." The uvular ȟ is produced far back in the throat, near the uvula — like a strong German "ch" in "Bach" but further back. This distinction changes meaning: he (that) vs. ȟe (a mountain/hill) are completely different words.

1.3 Diacritical Marks Summary

The NLD uses several diacritical marks that are essential to correct Lakota writing. Omitting or confusing them changes meaning.

Acute accent (á, é, í, ó, ú): Marks the stressed vowel — longer duration, higher pitch.

Háček (š, ž, č, ǧ, ȟ): Indicates specific consonant sounds. The háček on ȟ marks the uvular fricative (distinct from plain h), and on ǧ marks the voiced velar fricative.

Capital letters at word end (e.g., sápA, yÁ, šičÁ): In the NLD citation form, a capital final letter indicates an ablaut verb — one whose final vowel changes depending on what follows (see Section 3.3).

Nasal marker (ŋ): Follows a vowel to indicate nasalization: aŋ, iŋ, uŋ.

Apostrophe ('): Marks ejective consonants (p', t', k', č') and the glottal stop in certain morphological contexts (e.g., ič'í- reflexive prefix, náȟ'uŋ to hear).

1.4 Common Pronunciation Errors for English Speakers

English speakers typically struggle with five areas, all of which must be mastered for accurate Lakota:

Aspiration confusion: English speakers often pronounce plain stops with aspiration (since English "p" in "pin" is aspirated). In Lakota, plain p has no puff of air (like the "p" in "spin"), while pȟ has a strong puff. Practice by holding your hand in front of your mouth — you should feel a burst of air only on aspirated sounds.

Ejective difficulty: Ejective stops require closing the glottis (as in the middle of "uh-oh"), building air pressure, then releasing sharply. The result is a distinctive popping sound with no airflow from the lungs.

Nasal vowel approximation: English speakers tend to pronounce aŋ as the vowel a followed by a consonant n. In Lakota, the nasalization runs through the entire vowel — air passes through both the mouth and nose simultaneously throughout the vowel's duration.

Uvular fricative substitution: English speakers often pronounce ȟ as plain /h/ or velar /x/. The correct uvular ȟ requires the tongue root to constrict against the uvula, further back in the throat than the velar position.

Consonant cluster simplification: Lakota allows complex consonant clusters (like kȟn-, pȟl-) that English does not. Each consonant must be articulated clearly in sequence.

1.5 Prosodic Features

Stress patterns: Primary stress usually falls on the second syllable. In longer words, secondary stress occurs on alternating syllables. Stress can shift meaning in some words.

Intonation: Statements use falling intonation. Questions rise on the question word. Commands use sharp falling intonation. Emphasis is conveyed through high pitch on the stressed syllable.

Rhythm: Lakota is syllable-timed — each syllable takes roughly equal time, unlike the stress-timed rhythm of English. Stressed vowels are longer in duration.

k / kȟ / k':

ká (that over there) vs. kȟá (to dig) vs. k'á (rare)

č / čȟ / č':

wá (snow) vs. wáŋ (one, a)

čhí (to request) vs. čhíŋ (to want)

u vs. uŋ:

2. Core Grammar Foundations

2.1 Word Order and Sentence Structure

Lakota's basic word order is Subject-Object-Verb (SOV), the opposite of English (SVO):

Matȟó šúŋka kiŋ waŋyáŋka. — "The bear sees the dog." (Bear + dog + the + sees)

Hokšíla wóyute yúta. — "The boy eats food." (Boy + food + eats)

However, word order in Lakota is relatively free because grammatical relationships are marked on the verb itself through prefixes, not through word order. Rearranging words changes emphasis, not meaning:

Šúŋka kiŋ matȟó waŋyáŋka. — "The dog, the bear sees (it)." (emphasis on dog)

Waŋyáŋka matȟó šúŋka kiŋ. — "He sees — the bear — the dog." (emphasis on seeing)

↑ contents

2.2 The Verb-Centered Nature of Lakota

Unlike English, a single Lakota verb can constitute a complete sentence because all necessary information (who acts, upon whom, when, how) can be encoded in the verb through prefixes, suffixes, and enclitics:

Wabláka. — "I see (it/him/her)." (One word contains subject "I," action "see," and implied object)

Yá. — "Go!" or "He/she goes."

Wašté. — "(It) is good."

This makes verbs the absolute heart of Lakota grammar. Everything radiates outward from the verb.

↑ contents

2.3 Animate and Inanimate Distinctions

Lakota grammar distinguishes between animate and inanimate entities, which affects verb agreement, pronoun usage, and article choice:

Animate beings: Humans, animals, spirits, some natural phenomena (sun, moon, thunder), some plants and natural features.

Inanimate entities: Most objects, tools, food, some natural phenomena, abstract concepts.

The definite article kiŋ is used for animate or specifically referenced nouns, while kiŋ also serves inanimate nouns in most modern usage. Historically, some dialects used distinct forms.

↑ contents

2.4 Person Hierarchy

Lakota has a person hierarchy that affects transitive verb morphology:

1st person > 2nd person > 3rd person animate > 3rd person inanimate

When a higher-ranking person acts on a lower-ranking one, the verb takes one set of prefixes. When a lower-ranking person acts on a higher-ranking one, different forms are used. This is especially visible in the transitive person-marking system (Section 4.5).

2.5 Gender-Specific Speech

One of Lakota's most distinctive features is that the speaker's gender (not the listener's) is marked through sentence-final particles. This system operates primarily through assertion markers and command forms:

Male speakers use:

Particle Function Example
yeló Strong assertion Wašté yeló. "It's good!"
ló Oglala shortened assertion Hé wašté ló. "That's good." (everyday Oglala)
yo Command/request Hiyú yo. "Come!"
wo Command (variant form) Yúta wo! "Eat!"
hwo Formal question Wašté hwo? "Is it good?"
po Plural command Wakȟáŋyaŋ úŋ po. "Live in a sacred manner."

Female speakers use:

Particle Function Example
ye Command/assertion Hiyú ye. "Come!"
we Polite request/question Hiyú we.
kštó Strong assertion Wašté kštó. "It's good!"
pe Plural command Hiyú pe. "Come (all of you)!"
huŋwó Rhetorical question (male) ...héč thiyóspa huŋwó?

Gender-neutral particles that both speakers use: he (yes/no question marker), šni (negation), kte (future), s'a (habitual).

2.6 The Definite Article: kiŋ

Unlike English "the," the Lakota definite article kiŋ comes after the noun or noun phrase:

šúŋka kiŋ — "the dog"

wičháša kiŋ — "the man"

šúŋka tȟáŋka kiŋ — "the big dog" (descriptor comes between noun and article)

kiŋ also functions as a relativizer, creating relative clauses:

[Lowáŋ] wičháša kiŋ — "the man [who sings]"

[Wabláka] wičháša kiŋ — "the man [whom I saw]"

The previously-mentioned article k'uŋ refers to something already discussed: Wíŋyaŋ k'uŋ. — "The woman (the one we mentioned before)."

2.7 Demonstratives

Lakota demonstratives indicate spatial proximity:

Demonstrative Meaning Example
lé this (near speaker) Lé ičú yo. "Take this."
hé that (further away) Hé ičú yo. "Take that."
ká that (over there, far) Ká waŋyáŋka yo. "Look at that over there."
léna these Léna wašté. "These are good."
héna those Héna tȟáŋkapi. "Those are big."

2.8 Negation

The basic negation marker šni follows the verb:

Wašté šni. — "It is not good."

Hí šni. — "He didn't come."

For emphatic negation, Lakota pairs negative pronouns with šni:

Tákuni ...šni — "nothing/not anything": Tákuni slolyé šni. — "I don't know anything."

Tuwéni ...šni — "nobody/not anyone": Tuwéni hí šni. — "Nobody came."

Tóhinni ...šni — "never": Tóhinni ečhúŋ šni. — "He never does it."

Waníča — "there is none": Wóyute waníča. — "There is no food."

Negative commands (prohibitions) use the pattern verb + šni + command particle:

Male: Yá šni yo! — "Don't go!"

Female: Yá šni ye! — "Don't go!"

Plural: Yápi šni po! — "Don't go!" (to a group)

↑ contents

2.9 Question Formation

Yes/No Questions are formed by adding he at the end of a statement:

Wašté he? — "Is it good?"

Yahí he? — "Are you coming?"

Yútíŋ kte he? — "Will he eat?"

WH-Questions use interrogative words, typically placed at the beginning:

Question Word Meaning Example
táku what Táku yačhíŋ he? "What do you want?"
tuwé who Tuwé hé? "Who is that?"
tóhaŋ when Tóhaŋ yahí he? "When did you come?"
tókiya where (to) Tókiya lá he? "Where are you going?"
tuktél where (at) Tuktél yathí he? "Where do you live?"
tókheškhe how Tókheškhe ečháyaŋ he? "How did you do it?"
tóna how many Tóna duhá he? "How many do you have?"
tónakča how much (price) Lé tónakča he? "How much is this?"

Alternative questions use qaíš ("or"): Yá kte he qaíš yaŋké kte he? — "Will you go or will you stay?"

2.10 Pronoun Overview

Lakota does not have freestanding pronouns in the way English does. Instead, person is marked directly on the verb through prefixes. However, independent pronouns exist for emphasis:

Independent Pronouns (Emphatic):

Pronoun Meaning Usage
miyé I, me (emphatic) Miyé blá kte. "I (myself) will go."
niyé you (emphatic) Niyé lá he? "Are YOU going?"
iyé he/she/it (emphatic) Iyé hí. "HE came." (not someone else)
uŋkíye we (emphatic) Uŋkíye uŋyáŋpi kte. "WE will go."

Reflexive Pronouns:

miyé čha — "as for me, I myself"

niyé čha — "as for you, you yourself"

iyéš — "he/she himself/herself"

Demonstrative Pronouns (used independently):

lé — "this one" (near speaker)

hé — "that one" (further away)

ká — "that one over there" (distant)

léna — "these"

héna — "those"

These pronouns are used for emphasis. In normal Lakota speech, the verb prefix alone carries the person information and no separate pronoun is needed: Blá. = "I go." (no pronoun necessary). Adding miyé adds emphasis: Miyé blá. = "I go" / "It's me who goes."

3. Verb System: Classes, Conjugation, and Ablaut

The verb system is the core of Lakota grammar. Understanding verb classes, person-marking patterns, and the ablaut system is essential for speaking and reading Lakota accurately.

↑ contents

3.1 Stative Verbs

Stative verbs describe states, qualities, or conditions. What English expresses with adjectives, Lakota expresses with stative verbs. The word tȟáŋka doesn't mean "big" as a static adjective — it means "to be big," a complete predicate.

Stative verbs use ma-/ni- person-marking prefixes:

Person Prefix Example with wašté (be good)
1st singular ma- mawášte "I am good"
2nd singular ni- niwášte "you are good"
3rd singular ∅ wašté "he/she is good"
1st dual/plural uŋ(k)- uŋwaštépi "we are good"
2nd plural ni-...-pi niwaštépi "you all are good"
3rd plural ...-pi waštépi "they are good"

3.3 The Ablaut System

Ablaut is one of the most important grammatical features in Lakota. Certain verbs systematically change their final vowel depending on what follows them in the sentence. In NLD citation forms, these verbs are marked with a capital letter at the end (e.g., yÁ, sápA, šičÁ).

The Three Ablaut Forms

A-form (Basic/Citation Form): Used in simple declarative statements, negative statements with šni, questions with he, and past-time contexts.

Yá. — "He goes." / Yá šni. — "He doesn't go." / Yá he? — "Is he going?"

E-form (Emphatic/Connective): Used before the male emphatic yeló, the female assertion ye, the emphasis marker kštó, and certain other particles.

Yé yeló! — "He's going!" (male) / Yé kštó! — "He's really going!" (female)

Iŋ-form (Subordinate/Future): MUST be used before the future marker kte, in conditional clauses with kiŋháŋ, and in purpose clauses with héčhiŋ.

Yíŋ kte. — "He will go." / Yíŋ kiŋháŋ... — "If he goes..."

4. Advanced Prefix System

Lakota verbs carry an extraordinary amount of information through their prefix system. Understanding these prefixes transforms a learner from basic sentence construction to genuine fluency.

↑ contents

4.1 Locative/Directional Prefix Triad

Three core spatial prefixes form a coherent semantic system for indicating the spatial relationship between the action and a reference point.

a- Prefix (Surface Contact / General Locative)

Semantic range: "on, upon, at, against"

yaŋkÁ (sit) → ayáŋka (sit on something)

nážiŋ (stand) → anážiŋ (stand on something)

hpáya (fall) → ahpáya (fall onto something)

wáŋka (lie) → awáŋka (lie on something)

Extended into abstract meaning: blézA (be clear) → ablézA (realize, become clear about)

o- Prefix (Enclosure / Interior)

Semantic range: "in, into, within, through"

hnáka (place) → ohnáka (place into)

yaŋkÁ (sit) → oyáŋka (sit in)

máni (walk) → ománi (walk around in)

The o- prefix also creates nouns of location from verbs:

wótA (eat) → owóta (eating place, restaurant)

wayáwa (read) → owáyawa (reading place, school)

i- Prefix (Direction / Instrumentality)

Semantic range: "toward, to, at, by means of, with reference to"

yÁ (go) → iyÁ (go toward)

nážiŋ (stand) → inážiŋ (stand toward/facing)

čhéyA (cry) → ičhéyA (cry for/about)

Instrumental function: yútA (eat) → iyútA (eat with something as utensil)

4.2 Instrumental Prefixes

These prefixes indicate the instrument or means by which an action is performed. They attach to verb stems to create specific meanings:

Prefix Means/Instrument Example
wa- General instrument, by cutting apÁ → waápA (strike with something)
pa- By pushing, pressure blečÁ → pablečÁ (break by pushing)
ya- By mouth, biting ksa → yaksa (bite off)
ka- By striking, force blečÁ → kablečÁ (break by striking)
na- By foot, internal force blečÁ → nablečÁ (break by stepping on)
yu- By hand, pulling žáža → yužáža (wash by hand)

4.3 Valency-Modifying Prefixes

ki-/kič- (Benefactive/Dative Applicative)

Adds a beneficiary, maleficiary, or possessor argument to the verb:

káǧA → kičáǧA (make for someone)

ópȟetȟuŋ → kičhópȟetȟuŋ (buy for someone)

k'ú → kík'u (give back to someone)

manúŋ → kimanúŋ (steal from someone)

ič'i- (Reflexive/Auto-benefactive)

Subject acts on or for themselves:

yužáža → ič'íyužaža (wash oneself)

káǧA → ič'íkaǧa (make for oneself)

waŋyáŋkA → ič'íwaŋyaŋka (see oneself)

Phonological note: The glottal stop in ič'i- distinguishes it from iči- (with/by means of).

kičí- (Reciprocal)

Mutual action between multiple participants, requires plural subject marking:

ktÁ (kill) → kičíktapi (kill each other)

waštélakA (like) → kičíwaštElakapi (like each other)

4.5 Prefix Ordering Template

When combining multiple prefixes, Lakota follows strict ordering:

[Outer Locative] + [Inner Locative] + [Benefactive] + [Instrumental] + [Person] + STEM

Examples: a-ki-čáǧA = make on something for someone; o-ki-gnaka = place in something for someone.

4.6 Morphophonological Processes at Prefix Boundaries

When prefixes combine with stems, several regular sound changes occur:

Vowel coalescence: o + i → oi; a + u → au; i + a → iya

Consonant mutations: ki + V → kič: ki-áǧa → kičáǧa

Epenthesis (vowel insertion): Insert y between i and a: ki-áǧa → kiyáǧa; insert w between o and a.

Deletion: Delete a before a: wa-ayúta → wayúta

5.2 The kte Particle: Future and Beyond

kte is one of the most versatile particles in Lakota, expressing future actions, predictions, hypotheticals, intentions, and obligations:

Hí kte. — "He will come." (future)

Wašté kte. — "It would/might be good." (hypothetical)

Certainty levels can be modified with additional particles:

Hí kte séčA. — "He will probably come."

Hí kte načhéče. — "He will likely come."

Awíčhakhehaŋ hí kte. — "He will definitely come."

5.3 Conditional and Temporal Clauses: kiŋháŋ

kiŋháŋ serves as both "if" (conditional) and "when" (temporal), depending on context:

These simplified spellings are unfortunately common on social media and informal writing. Applying English Phonological Rules to Lakota

English speakers automatically aspirate voiceless stops at the beginning of words (pin = [pʰɪn]). In Lakota, initial plain stops are truly unaspirated. The word pa (head) should sound like the p in English "spa," NOT like the p in "pa."

Similarly, English speakers voice consonants between vowels (American English "butter" → "budder"). Lakota consonants maintain their exact quality regardless of position.

12. Oglala Dialect Features

This section documents specific features of the Oglala (Southern Lakota) dialect as spoken at Pine Ridge and surrounding communities. These features distinguish Oglala speech from Northern Lakota varieties (Standing Rock, Cheyenne River) and from the broader Dakota-Nakota continuum.

↑ contents

12.1 The ló / yeló Distinction

The most immediately recognizable Oglala marker is the shortened male assertion particle ló where Northern Lakota speakers typically use the full form yeló.

Oglala (Pine Ridge): Héčhetu ló. — "That's right!"

Northern Lakota (Standing Rock): Héčhetu yeló. — "That's right!"

Oglala: Wašté ló. — "It's good!"

Northern: Wašté yeló. — "It's good!"

Oglala: Makȟákiže ló, waní wačhíŋ yeló. — "I am suffering, I want to live."

Northern: Makȟákiže yeló, waní wačhíŋ yeló. — (same, but full form throughout)

The shortened ló carries the same assertive force as the full yeló and is the standard form in everyday Oglala speech. In more formal or ceremonial contexts, some Oglala speakers may use the full yeló for heightened gravity.

Usage environments for Oglala ló:

Hí kte ló. — "He will come!" (assertion after future marker)

Líla wašté ló. — "It's very good!" (assertion after stative)

Blá kte ló. — "I'm going to go!" (assertion with Class 2 verb)

Lowáŋ uŋ ló. — "He's singing!" (assertion after progressive)

Wabláka ló. — "I saw it!" (assertion after transitive)

Important: The female assertion markers kštó and ye do not undergo this same shortening in Oglala speech. Oglala women use kštó and ye in the same way as speakers from other Lakota communities.

↑ contents

12.2 Oglala Phonological Features

Several phonological features mark Oglala speech as distinct from other Lakota varieties:

Vowel quality in unstressed syllables: Oglala speakers tend to reduce unstressed vowels more than Northern speakers, particularly in rapid speech. The unstressed a may sound closer to a schwa [ə] in casual Oglala conversation.

Contraction patterns: Oglala conversational speech shows frequent phonological contractions that are less common in Northern varieties or formal speech:

wanáǧi → wanáǧ' (spirit — final vowel elision in rapid speech)

héčhetu → héčhtu (that's so — middle vowel compression)

nakáha → nakȟá (now — syllable reduction)

Consonant cluster tolerance: Oglala speakers maintain complex consonant clusters faithfully (e.g., kȟn-, pȟl-, mn-), which is consistent across Lakota varieties, but Oglala speakers may produce slightly stronger aspiration on aspirated consonants compared to Northern varieties.

12.4 Oglala Enclitic Ordering Tendencies

Oglala speakers show a preference for placing ló at the very end of a complex enclitic chain, after all other modifying particles. This creates a characteristic cadence:

Hí kte ló. — "He'll come!" (future + Oglala assertion)

Yúta uŋ kte ló. — "He'll be eating!" (progressive + future + Oglala assertion)

Wašté šni ló. — "It's not good!" (negative + Oglala assertion)

Compared to the Northern pattern where yeló occupies the same position but with its fuller phonological weight, Oglala sentences have a characteristically clipped, emphatic ending.

↑ contents

12.5 Ceremonial Register in Oglala

In Oglala ceremonial contexts (Inípi, Haŋbléčheyapi, Wiwáŋyaŋg Wačhípi), the language shifts toward a more conservative register that may employ the full yeló even among speakers who normally use ló in everyday speech. Key ceremonial phrases maintain traditional forms:

Čhaŋnúŋpa kiŋ lé uȟá hóye wayé ló. — "With this pipe I am sending a voice." (Oglala form)

Tȟuŋkášila, waŋmáyaŋka yo. — "Grandfather, behold me." (unchanged across dialects)

Makȟákiže ló, waní wačhíŋ yeló. — "I am suffering, I want to live." (note: ló in first clause, full yeló in second for emphasis — a common Oglala ceremonial pattern)

The Pine Ridge ceremonial tradition, particularly as preserved by the Chips family lineage (Benjamin Godfrey Chipps Sr. and descendants), maintains specific song and prayer forms that represent the Oglala standard for these registers.

12.6 Oglala vs. Sičháŋǧu (Rosebud) Differences

Though both are Southern Lakota, minor differences exist between Oglala (Pine Ridge) and Sičháŋǧu (Rosebud) speech:

Oglala tends toward ló while Sičháŋǧu speakers may alternate between ló and yeló more freely.

Sičháŋǧu speech, particularly in the tradition of Albert White Hat Sr., sometimes preserves slightly more archaic vocabulary choices that Oglala has updated.

The kinship vocabulary is identical, but the address patterns (who calls whom by what term, and when) may vary slightly between Pine Ridge and Rosebud communities based on family tradition.

13. Noun Morphology and Postpositions

13.1 Noun Formation from Verbs

Lakota forms many nouns from verbs using systematic prefixes and suffixes:

wa- + verb stem = agent/instrument noun:

lowáŋ (sing) → walówaŋ (singer)

wačhí (dance) → wawáčhi (dancer)

yáwa (read) → wayáwa (reader, student)

kté (kill) → wakté (killer, warrior)

o- + verb stem = place/result noun:

wóta (eat) → owóte (eating place, restaurant)

yáwa (read) → owáyawa (school, reading place)

yaŋkÁ (sit) → oyáŋke (a seat, a place to sit)

wáŋka (lie down) → owáŋka (a bed, sleeping place)

thí (to dwell) → othí (a dwelling area)

háŋ (to stand) → oháŋ (an area where something stands)

wo- + verb stem = abstract noun / result:

ksápa (wise) → wóksape (wisdom)

wašté (good) → wówašte (goodness, virtue)

ohítikA (brave) → wóohitike (bravery)

ečhúŋ (do) → wóečhuŋ (a deed, an act)

čhéyA (cry) → wóčheye (a cry, lamentation)

kȟízA (fight) → wókȟize (a battle, war)

wi- + verb stem = instrument/means noun:

iyútA (measure) → wíyute (a measuring tool)

pázA (point) → wípaze (a pointer, index)

14. Relative Clauses and Complex Sentences

14.1 Relative Clause Formation with kiŋ

Lakota forms relative clauses by placing the modifying clause before the noun and following it with the definite article kiŋ, which functions as both determiner and relativizer:

[Lowáŋ] wičháša kiŋ — "The man [who sings]"

[Wabláka] wičháša kiŋ — "The man [whom I saw]"

[Wóyute yúta] hokšíla kiŋ — "The boy [who eats the food]"

[Líla lúzahaŋ] šúŋka kiŋ — "The dog [that is very fast]"

[Htálehaŋ wahí] otȟúŋwahe kiŋ — "The town [that I came to yesterday]"

[Wabláka] šúŋka kiŋ tȟáŋka. — "The dog that I saw is big."

14.2 Complement Clauses

Verbs of thinking, saying, and knowing take complement clauses:

With keyÁ (to say):

15.10 Enclitics and Particles: Expanded Usage Notes

The séčA / načhéče / škhé Distinction in Practice

These three evidential markers encode the speaker's relationship to the information. Mastering them is essential for sounding natural and being truthful in Lakota:

séčA — uncertainty, possibility ("maybe, perhaps"):

Hí kte séčA. — "He might come." (I'm not sure)

Maǧážu kte séčA. — "It might rain." (uncertain)

Wašté séčA. — "Maybe it's good." (hedging)

načhéče — inference from evidence ("probably, apparently"):

Hí kte načhéče. — "He'll probably come." (I have reason to think so)

Maǧážu kte načhéče. — "It will probably rain." (dark clouds visible)

Makȟúža načhéče. — "He's apparently sick." (I see evidence of it)

škhé — hearsay, reported information ("I hear, they say"):

Hí škhé. — "He came, I hear." (someone told me)

Maǧážu kte škhé. — "They say it'll rain." (weather report / someone's word)

Líla wašté škhé. — "It's really good, they say." (secondhand)

In Oglala conversation, these markers are used constantly and carry significant weight. Using načhéče when you should use škhé implies you have direct evidence when you actually only have hearsay — a form of overstatement that traditional Lakota speakers notice.

The na / čha / uŋkáŋ Conjunction System

These three connectors serve different discourse functions:

na — simple "and," sequential events:

Wahí na iyótaka. — "I came and sat down." (two sequential events)

Lowáŋ na wačhí. — "He sings and dances." (two simultaneous or linked activities)

čha — "so, therefore" (cause-result):

Malokhíŋ čha wayúte kte. — "I'm hungry so I'll eat."

Líla makȟáta čha tȟatéhiŋyela wašté. — "It's really hot so a breeze would be nice."

uŋkáŋ — "and then, whereupon" (unexpected/narrative transition):

Máni uŋkáŋ matȟó waŋ wabláka. — "I was walking and then I saw a bear." (unexpected event)

Iwáŋka uŋkáŋ hóthúŋiŋyaŋ waŋ naháŋ'uŋ. — "I was lying down and then I heard a noise."

uŋkáŋ is the essential storytelling connector — it moves a narrative forward with a sense of discovery and surprise. Traditional stories are full of this particle.

18. Lakota Worldview Through Language

18.1 The Sacred Circle in Language

The concept of the circle (čhaŋgléška) permeates Lakota language at every level. The grammatical structure itself reflects circular thinking — verbs return to their base forms, ceremonial language circles through the four directions, and kinship terminology creates webs of reciprocal relationship rather than linear hierarchies.

In conversation, Lakota speakers often circle back to their main point rather than proceeding linearly. A story may begin with a conclusion, explore its context, and arrive back at the conclusion with enriched understanding. This is reflected in the common narrative particle uŋkáŋ ("and then, whereupon") which moves the listener through temporal circles rather than straight lines.

↑ contents

18.2 Animacy and Relatedness

The animate/inanimate distinction in Lakota grammar is not merely a grammatical feature — it reflects a worldview in which many things that English considers "objects" are understood as living beings with spirit:

The Sun (Wí), Moon (Haŋhépi Wí), Stars (Wičháȟpi), and Thunder Beings (Wakȟíŋyaŋ) are all grammatically animate and spiritually active agents.

Íŋyaŋ (the Rock) is one of the oldest and most sacred beings — animate, ancient, and the material foundation of the Inípi ceremony.

When a Lakota speaker says Makȟá kiŋ lé Uŋčí uŋkítȟawapi — "This Earth is our Grandmother," they are not speaking metaphorically. The grammatical and spiritual systems align: Uŋčí Makȟá (Grandmother Earth) is grammatically animate, addressed with kinship terms, and treated with the same respect as a human grandmother.

↑ contents

18.3 The Evidential System and Truth

Lakota's evidential markers (škhé, načhéče, séčA, keyÁ) encode not just what a speaker says but how they know it. This system reflects a deep cultural commitment to honesty about the source of one's knowledge:

Matȟó hí. — "A bear came." (speaker witnessed it)

Matȟó hí škhé. — "A bear came, I heard." (hearsay — speaker didn't see it)

Matȟó hí načhéče. — "A bear probably came." (inference from evidence)

Matȟó hí séčA. — "A bear might have come." (uncertainty)

Matȟó hí keyá. — "He said a bear came." (reported speech)

In Oglala tradition, claiming to have seen something you only heard about is considered a form of dishonesty. The evidential system builds this ethical commitment directly into the grammar.

↑ contents

18.4 Relationship Vocabulary and Social Structure

The extensive kinship vocabulary of Lakota is not merely a naming system — it encodes an entire social structure of obligations, responsibilities, and behavioral expectations. Knowing what to call someone in Lakota means knowing exactly how to behave toward them.

The term kȟolá (a close male friend) creates a bond that carries lifelong obligations of mutual support, honesty, and sacrifice. When someone addresses you as kȟolá, they are invoking a relationship as binding as kinship.

The term thiyóšpaye (extended family, band) reminds speakers that identity is never individual — you exist within a network of relationships that define who you are. The first question Lakota people ask each other when meeting is often not "What's your name?" but Tuwé nitȟíyošpaye he? — "Who is your extended family?" or Tuktél yaúŋ he? — "Where do you live/come from?" — questions that locate a person within a web of relationships.

↑ contents

18.5 The Power of Naming: Wóičhaže

In Lakota tradition, names carry spiritual power. A person's Lakota name (wóičhaže) is not merely a label but a description of their spiritual identity, often given through ceremony or dreams. Speaking a name invokes the qualities and spiritual forces embedded in it.

This is reflected grammatically in the naming construction:

___ emáčiyapi. — "They call me ___." (Literally: "They call me by ___." — The passive construction reflects that a name is something conferred upon you by the community, not something you choose for yourself.)

Tókhes enáčiyapi he? — "What do they call you?" (How are you named?)

↑ contents

18.6 Language as Resistance and Reclamation

For Oglala people at Pine Ridge, speaking Lakota is not merely a linguistic exercise — it is an act of cultural sovereignty and resistance against centuries of forced assimilation. The boarding school era (1870s–1960s) deliberately targeted Lakota language for elimination, punishing children for speaking their own language. Every Lakota word spoken today carries the weight of survival.

The phrase Lakȟótiyapi iyá yo — "Speak Lakota!" — is both a command and a declaration of resistance. When elders say Lakȟótiyapi kiŋ lé wakȟáŋ — "The Lakota language is sacred" — they are describing a spiritual reality, not making a metaphor.

Key expressions of linguistic sovereignty:

Lakȟótiyapi kiŋ uŋkítȟawapi. — "The Lakota language is ours."

Iyápi uŋkítȟawapi kiŋ wakȟáŋ. — "Our language is sacred."

Uŋčhíŋčala kiŋ Lakȟótiyapi uŋspéwičhauŋkȟiyapi kte. — "We will teach our children Lakota."

Wakȟáŋyaŋ iyáya yo. — "Speak in a sacred manner."

Ehánni iyápi uŋkíyapi s'a kiŋ hé akhé uŋkíyapi kte. — "The language we used to speak long ago, we will speak again."

The act of creating and using this guide is itself an act of reclamation — taking the tools of linguistic documentation and turning them toward the preservation and revitalization of a living language that belongs to the Oglala Lakota people.

19. Reduplication Patterns

Reduplication — repeating all or part of a word — is a productive morphological process in Lakota that creates new meanings from existing roots. Understanding reduplication is essential for both comprehension and natural-sounding speech.

↑ contents

19.1 Full Reduplication

Full reduplication repeats the entire root. The semantic effect depends on the word class and context.

Stative verbs (adjectives) — attenuation ("somewhat, -ish"):

šá (red) → šašá (reddish, somewhat red)

sápa (black) → sasápa (blackish, dark-ish)

lúta (scarlet) → lulúta (reddish)

zí (yellow) → zizí (yellowish)

ská (white) → skaská (whitish)

tȟó (blue/green) → tȟotȟó (bluish/greenish)

Note the pattern: full reduplication of color terms reduces intensity rather than increasing it. This is the opposite of what English speakers might expect — "reddish" is less red than "red."

Stative verbs — intensity or plurality:

tȟáŋka (big) → tȟatȟáŋka (big ones, various big things)

čík'ala (small) → čičík'ala (very small, tiny)

háŋska (tall) → haháŋska (various tall ones)

wašté (good) → wawašté (various good things)

Here the effect shifts from attenuation to distributive plurality — "various X ones" rather than "somewhat X."

↑ contents

19.2 Partial Reduplication (CV- Pattern)

The most common partial reduplication copies the first consonant-vowel sequence of the root. This typically creates repetitive or iterative meaning with active verbs.

Active verbs — repeated action:

psíča (jump) → psipsíča (jump repeatedly, hop around)

náži (stand) → nanáži (stand up repeatedly, keep standing up)

iyáya (go away) → iyiyáya (go back and forth)

hiyú (come) → hihiyú (keep coming, come repeatedly)

kté (kill) → kikté (kill many, kill repeatedly)

Stative verbs — diminutive effect:

šá (red) → šašá (pinkish, pale red)

zí (yellow) → zizí (light yellow)

ǧí (brown) → ǧiǧí (tannish, light brown)

↑ contents

19.3 CVC- Reduplication Pattern

Some words reduplicate with a consonant-vowel-consonant pattern, often creating intensified or vivid imagery.

xá (to be unraveled) → xaxá (thoroughly unraveled)

pȟá (to be bitter) → pȟapȟá (intensely bitter)

bléča (shattered) → blébléča (completely shattered, in many pieces)

↑ contents

19.4 Semantic Effects Summary

The meaning of reduplication depends on verb class and context:

With active verbs: Repeated or continuous action — psipsíča "jumping repeatedly"

With stative verbs (colors): Attenuation — šašá "somewhat red, reddish"

With stative verbs (size/quality): Distributive plurality — tȟatȟáŋka "various big ones"

With some roots: Diminutive — čičík'ala "very small, tiny"

With onomatopoeia: Sustained sound — hoŋhoŋ "barking continuously"

kakhí iyéya — "brought back and threw (set down)"

káǧa ihúni — "finished making"

lowáŋ ihúni — "finished singing"

Full sentence examples:

Até waŋyáŋg iyáya. — "Father went to see."

Wóphiye kiŋ kahí yušná. — "He brought the box and dropped it."

Wičhíŋčala kiŋ lowáŋ ihúni. — "The girl finished singing."

20.3 Action + Manner Chains

These describe how an action is performed by pairing the main verb with a manner expression.

čhaŋté wašté máni — "walks happily" (good-heartedly walks)

ohítiya íŋyaŋka — "runs hard/bravely"

iwášteya lowáŋ — "sings beautifully"

ikpíza ečhúŋ — "does it carefully/seriously"

Full sentence examples:

Kȟoškálaka kiŋ ohítiya íŋyaŋka. — "The young man runs hard."

Uŋčí iwášteya lowáŋ. — "Grandmother sings beautifully."

20.4 Auxiliary Verb Chains

Lakota uses auxiliary-like verbs in chains to mark aspect, modality, and evidentiality.

Ability: okíhi — Lakȟótiyapi íya okíhi. "He can speak Lakota."

Desire: čhíŋ — Wóyute yúta čhíŋ. "He wants to eat food."

Obligation: iyéčheča — Yá iyéčheča. "He should go."

Beginning: hiŋglé — Škáta hiŋglé. "He started to play."

Completion: ihúni — Wayáwa ihúni. "He finished reading."

Habitual: s'a — Aŋpétu iyóhi hí s'a. "He comes every day."

Continuous: uŋ — Lowáŋ uŋ. "He is singing."

20.5 Three-Verb Chains

Fluent speakers regularly produce chains of three or more verbs, particularly in narrative speech.

Glí na iyótake na wóyute yúta. — "He came home, sat down, and ate food."

Waŋyáŋg iyáya na kakhí hí. — "He went to look and came back."

Čhéya šná na ištíŋma iyáya. — "She cried continuously and fell asleep."

Škáta čhíŋ tkȟá okíhi šni. — "He wants to play but can't."

20.6 Verb Chains in Storytelling

In traditional Lakota narrative, verb chains create rhythm and momentum. A storyteller might say:

Matȟó waŋ hí na háŋ na étunwaŋ na héčhel iyáya. — "A bear came and stood there and looked around and then went on."

The chaining with na ("and") creates a flowing sequence of events characteristic of oral tradition.

21. Ceremonial Song Language: Directional Prayers and Song Patterns

This section documents recurring linguistic patterns found across the sacred song traditions, with attention to how ceremonial language differs from everyday speech. This vocabulary is presented for educational purposes — proper ceremonial use requires cultural guidance.

↑ contents

21.1 The Six Directions in Ceremonial Language

Ceremonies address six directions, each with specific vocabulary. The directional terms used in songs often differ slightly from everyday speech forms.

Wiyóȟpeyata — the West (where the sun goes down; the Thunder Beings)

Wazíyata — the North (the White Giant; endurance and purification)

Wiyóhiyaŋpata — the East (where the sun rises; new understanding)

Itókagata — the South (the direction of warmth and growth)

Waŋkátakiya — Above (Wakȟáŋ Tȟáŋka, the sky realm)

Makȟátakiya — Below / the Earth (Uŋčí Makȟá, Grandmother Earth)

In song, the directional suffixes -taŋhaŋ (from) and -kiya (toward) are essential:

Wiyóȟpeyata kiyá hóye wayé ló. — "Toward the west I send a voice."

Wazíyata kiŋyáŋ itúŋwaŋ yo. — "To the north, look!"

Waŋkátakiya hóye wayé ló. — "Upward I send a voice."

↑ contents

21.2 Recurring Song Phrases and Their Grammar

Several phrases appear across many ceremonial song types. Understanding their grammar deepens both comprehension and respect.

Hóye wayé ló — "I am sending a voice" (hóye = a calling/voice; wa-yé = I-send; ló = male assertion, Oglala form)

Čhaŋnúŋpa waŋ uhá hóye wayé ló. — "With a pipe I am sending a voice."

Tatúye tópa kiyá hóye wayé ló. — "To the four winds I am sending a voice."

Waŋmáyaŋka yo — "Behold me" / "Look upon me" (waŋ-má-yaŋka = see-me; yo = male imperative)

Tȟuŋkášila, waŋmáyaŋka yo. — "Grandfather, look upon me."

Ahítowanyo — "Behold me" / "Sit there watching" (a contracted ceremonial form)

Ahítowanyo, ahítowanyo. — "Behold me, behold me." (Sun Dance opening invocation)

Oŋšímala ye — "Have pity on me" (oŋší-ma-la = pity-me; ye = female imperative, but in prayer used regardless of speaker gender to show humility)

Wakȟáŋ Tȟáŋka, oŋšímala ye. — "Great Mystery, have pity on me."

Makȟákiže ló — "I am suffering" (ceremonial acknowledgment of human limitation)

Makȟákiže ló, waní wačhíŋ yeló. — "I am suffering, I want to live."

Mitákuye ob waní kte ló — "With my relatives I shall live" (the affirmation of communal existence)

↑ contents

21.3 Four Directions Song Patterns (Tatúye Tópa Olówaŋ)

The Four Directions songs follow consistent linguistic patterns. Each direction is addressed in sequence, typically starting from the West (Wiyóȟpeyata).

Pattern 1 — Calling and praying:

Wiyóȟpeyata kiŋyáŋ — itúŋwaŋ yo, čhekíya yo, čhekíya yo — hiná nitáku yeló.

"To the west — look, pray, pray — those are your relatives."

Wazíyata kiŋyáŋ — itúŋwaŋ yo, čhekíya yo, čhekíya yo — hiná nitáku yeló.

"To the north — look, pray, pray — those are your relatives."

This pattern moves through all six directions: West, North, East, South, Above (Waŋkátaŋ), and Earth (Makȟátaŋ).

Pattern 2 — Stone nation coming:

Wiyóȟpeyata tȟuŋkáŋ oyáte waŋ waníyaŋ uwé ló. — "To the west a stone nation is coming, you will live."

Wazíyata tȟuŋkáŋ oyáte waŋ waníyaŋ uwé ló. — "To the north a stone nation is coming, you will live."

This references the heated stones (tȟuŋkáŋ) brought into the Inípi lodge.

↑ contents

21.4 Grammar Notes on Ceremonial Language

Ceremonial language differs from everyday Lakota in several ways:

Archaic forms: Some verb forms and particles preserved in songs are no longer common in daily speech, such as uwé ("is coming," archaic progressive).

Gender markers in prayer: The imperative ye (typically female speech) appears in prayers regardless of speaker gender, conveying humility and petition. Yo (male imperative) is used when addressing directly.

Contracted forms: Song meter requires contracted or shortened words: ahítowanyo compresses ahítuŋwaŋ yaŋké ló yo ("sit there watching, look!").

Vocable patterns: Many songs include vocable syllables (heyúye, heyáya) that carry melodic and spiritual function without direct lexical meaning. These are not "meaningless" — they are integral to the song's spiritual power.

22.3 Counting in Practical Context

Counting years (waníyetu = winter = year):

Waníyetu wikčémna núŋpa sám záptaŋ mayápi. — "I am twenty-five years old." (literally: "winters twenty-five I have")

Counting days (aŋpétu = day):

Aŋpétu yámni héčhel wahí kte. — "In three days I'll come."

Counting money (mázaska = money/silver):

Mázaska opáwiŋǧe žéča yačhíŋ he? — "Do you want one hundred dollars?"

Counting people:

Wičháša záptaŋ hípi. — "Five men came."

Wiŋyáŋ núŋpa hél yaŋkápi. — "Two women are sitting there."

22.4 The Sacred Number Four (Tópa)

The number four (tópa) holds central ceremonial significance in Lakota culture. It organizes the spiritual and physical world:

Tatúye Tópa — The Four Winds/Directions

Wičhóȟ'aŋ Wakȟáŋ Šakówiŋ — The Seven Sacred Rites (šakówiŋ = seven, also sacred)

Tiyópa Tópa — The Four Doors of Inípi (each round of the Sweat Lodge ceremony)

In ceremony, things are done in sets of four: four songs, four rounds, four prayers to the four directions. Even the number seven (šakówiŋ) carries weight — seven sacred rites, seven campfire circles of the Lakota nation.
